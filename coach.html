The Eagle DS Coaching Philosophy

The Programme

Built on
Outcomes.

The Certificate in Combat Sports Coaching (Applied) is a competency-based programme designed to develop capable, standards-driven coaches. It's inclusive by design — no prior formal coaching qualification required, only a commitment to the standard.

Delivered as a blended model: guided worksheets and facilitated discussion in the classroom, technique demonstration and observed coaching on the mat.

Four Core Areas

01Technical coaching capability across striking and grappling disciplines.
02Athlete development frameworks built on measurable progression standards.
03Performance-based assessment with transparent, fixed criteria.
04Communication, leadership and coaching business skills.

The Course · Week by Week

Five Weeks.
One Standard.

Each week builds the knowledge, the mindset, and the ability to communicate value — to parents, students, and the wider community.

Week 1
Gradings, Mindset & Messaging
The grading pathway, the outcome-based mindset, and how to communicate the system's value honestly.
Week 2
Standards
Teaching and enforcing technical excellence. You are the standard — you cannot enforce what you cannot perform.
Weeks 3–4
Fundamentals & Coaching Kids
The detail inside the technique, and why coaching kids requires more, not less. Treat their outcomes as your own.

Operational Definitions
If you cannot define it, you cannot teach it. Building definitions that create real understanding, not just labels.
Future

Supervised coaching, competition preparation, athlete welfare, and programme delivery — the full certificate pathway.

The Youth Academy

From Student
to Coach.

The Youth Academy is where advanced students train to coach junior students — building leadership, communication and certified responsibility. A rangatahi who now develops others.

It's the third stage of the Eagle DS pathway, and the most powerful: a young person who enters as someone the system struggled to reach, and leaves as a certified coach developing the next generation.

Why It Matters

When you coach a young person well — hold the standard, invest in their development, celebrate their progress — you're not just producing a better athlete. You're showing them what it looks like when an adult takes their potential seriously.

For some of them, it's the first time anyone has held them to a standard and believed they could meet it. That's the work — and it matters far beyond the mat.
